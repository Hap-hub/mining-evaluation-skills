# Risk Assessment Framework

Mining project risk is best organized into four categories. When building or reviewing a risk assessment, work through all four rather than defaulting to the geological risks that are usually most top-of-mind for geologists, or the market risks most top-of-mind for investors — the categories that get skipped are often where the real surprises come from.

## 1. Geological risk

- Resource confidence (proportion of Inferred vs. Indicated/Measured; drill spacing relative to deposit continuity/complexity).
- Grade variability and nugget effect (relevant especially for gold, and coarse-gold deposits in particular, where sample representativity is a known challenge).
- Structural complexity (faulting, folding) that could disrupt continuity assumed in the model.
- Metallurgical variability across the deposit (recovery may not be uniform — domain-specific testwork matters).

## 2. Technical risk

- Mining method suitability (open pit vs. underground; is the selected method appropriate for the geometry and depth of the deposit?).
- Processing/metallurgical risk — is the flowsheet proven for this ore type, or does it rely on novel/unproven technology at commercial scale?
- Infrastructure gaps (power, water, tailings storage, transport) that add capex/schedule risk not always reflected in early-stage capex estimates.
- Water balance and tailings management — increasingly a primary technical/permitting risk, not a footnote.

## 3. Market risk

- Commodity price volatility and the study's price deck (is it consistent with consensus forecasts, or optimistic relative to them?).
- Concentrate/product marketability — smelter/refinery terms, payability, treatment/refining charges, especially for polymetallic or unusual concentrate chemistries (e.g., high-arsenic copper concentrates face real market access constraints).
- Currency risk (costs typically local currency, revenue typically USD-denominated).
- Offtake/contract concentration risk.

## 4. Environmental, social, and governance (ESG) / permitting risk

- Permitting timeline and jurisdictional track record (some jurisdictions have long, uncertain, or politically contested permitting processes).
- Water usage and tailings storage facility design/failure risk (a major driver of both environmental and reputational risk industry-wide since several high-profile tailings failures).
- Community/Indigenous engagement and social license to operate — increasingly a determinant of project timeline, not just a compliance checkbox.
- Closure and reclamation cost provisioning — is it realistically estimated and bonded, or understated?
- Carbon intensity / energy source, increasingly relevant to financing access as lenders apply ESG screens.

See `references/esg-sustainability.md` for more depth on this category.

## How to structure a risk output

For a written risk assessment, a simple and effective structure per risk item is:

```
Risk: [short description]
Category: [geological / technical / market / environmental-social]
Likelihood: [low / medium / high]
Impact: [low / medium / high — ideally tied to what it would do to NPV/schedule if realized]
Mitigation / monitoring: [what's being done or should be done]
```

Avoid producing a risk list that's just a list of worries with no likelihood/impact/mitigation structure — that format doesn't help a reader prioritize, which is the entire point of a risk assessment. `assets/templates/risk_assessment_matrix.md` has a ready structure for this.

When reviewing a project, actively look for risks the user's material doesn't mention rather than only elaborating on the ones they raised — omissions (e.g., no discussion of tailings management for a project that clearly needs a TSF, or no mention of water rights in an arid jurisdiction) are often more informative than what's explicitly flagged.
