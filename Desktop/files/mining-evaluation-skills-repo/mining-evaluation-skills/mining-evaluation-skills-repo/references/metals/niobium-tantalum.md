# Niobium & Tantalum — Evaluation Notes

**Two distinct commodities frequently found together** in the same deposit types (both are found in carbonatites and in rare-metal pegmatites), but with very different markets — treat them as separate economic drivers even when the resource is reported jointly.

**Niobium (Nb)**: highly concentrated supply — one country (Brazil) and effectively one or two companies account for the overwhelming majority of global niobium supply, making this one of the most supply-concentrated commodities covered here. This concentration is itself a major market/strategic factor: new niobium projects are often evaluated as much on their "supply diversification" narrative to governments/customers as on pure cost-curve economics. **Deposit type**: primarily carbonatite-hosted (as pyrochlore), also present in some alkaline igneous complexes. **Uses**: overwhelmingly as ferroniobium for high-strength low-alloy (HSLA) steel — demand is closely tied to steel/infrastructure/automotive cycles.

**Tantalum (Ta)**: sourced from rare-metal pegmatites (as tantalite/columbite-tantalite, often called "coltan" when mined artisanally) and as a by-product of tin mining in some jurisdictions. **Conflict mineral status**: tantalum is one of the "3TG" conflict minerals (tin, tantalum, tungsten, gold) subject to specific supply chain due diligence requirements (e.g., under Dodd-Frank Section 1502 in the US, and EU Conflict Minerals Regulation) — any tantalum project evaluation, especially sourced from Central Africa, should explicitly address conflict-mineral due diligence and traceability (e.g., ITSCI or similar chain-of-custody schemes), not treat it as a standard base-metal supply chain question.

**Grade/reporting units**: niobium resources are typically reported as % Nb2O5; tantalum as ppm or % Ta2O5. Because both can occur in the same pegmatite or carbonatite body, check whether a resource statement is reporting a combined Nb-Ta grade or genuinely separate, independently-estimated grades — combined reporting without a clear split can obscure which metal is actually driving the project's economics.

**Market factors**: both are specialty/minor metals without deep, transparent public exchange pricing (unlike copper or gold) — pricing is typically via producer contracts or specialist trade publications, so a project's assumed price should be checked for its actual source/date rather than assumed to reflect a liquid market price; search for current specialist pricing sources when this matters to an evaluation.

**Common evaluation pitfalls**: not addressing conflict-mineral due diligence for tantalum sourced from high-risk jurisdictions; using illiquid/dated pricing assumptions without disclosing the source; conflating combined Nb+Ta grade with genuine dual-commodity economic contribution.
