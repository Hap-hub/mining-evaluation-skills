# Rare Earth Elements (REE) — Evaluation Notes

**Not one commodity — a basket of 17 elements with very different values and uses.** The single most important thing to check first in any REE project evaluation is the **magnet REE content and the light/heavy split**, because a "large REE resource" headline number can be almost meaningless without this breakdown:

- **Light REE (LREE)**: lanthanum, cerium, praseodymium, neodymium, samarium — cerium and lanthanum are the most abundant but lowest-value; **neodymium (Nd) and praseodymium (Pr)** are the high-value "magnet REEs" that drive most project economics (used in NdFeB permanent magnets for EV motors, wind turbines).
- **Heavy REE (HREE)**: europium, terbium, dysprosium, yttrium, and others — generally rarer, higher-value, and more supply-constrained than LREE; **dysprosium (Dy) and terbium (Tb)** are particularly critical magnet-performance additives (heat resistance) and command significant premiums. A project with meaningful HREE content is generally a materially better story than an LREE-only project, all else equal.

**Deposit types**: carbonatite-hosted (most large REE deposits, e.g., Bayan Obo-style, Mountain Pass-style — typically LREE-dominant), ion-adsorption clay deposits (mainly southern China-style — notable for being HREE-enriched and easier/cheaper to process via simple leaching, which is part of why China's HREE supply position is so structurally strong), and monazite/xenotime in placer or hard-rock settings.

**Grade reporting**: watch for whether a "TREO%" (Total Rare Earth Oxide) headline grade is disclosed alongside the actual distribution of value across individual elements — a high TREO% dominated by cerium and lanthanum (the least valuable REEs) can be a much weaker project than a lower TREO% deposit with strong Nd-Pr-Dy-Tb content. Always ask for or estimate the basket price / value distribution, not just headline TREO%.

**Processing is the hardest part of an REE project, not the mining.** Separating individual REEs from each other (they're chemically very similar) requires complex, multi-stage solvent extraction circuits that are capital-intensive, technically demanding, and where the large majority of global processing/separation capacity is concentrated in China. A REE project's economics hinge heavily on whether it has (or credibly plans) its own separation capacity, or depends on selling a mixed concentrate to a processor (typically at a significant value discount, and with the supply-chain/geopolitical exposure that implies) — this is usually the single most important thing to clarify in an REE project evaluation.

**Market/strategic context**: REEs (especially the magnet REEs) are widely treated as critical/strategic minerals by the US, EU, Japan, and others given supply concentration risk and their role in defense and clean-energy applications — many REE projects outside China are evaluated partly on government support/offtake potential (grants, loans, strategic stockpiling programs) as much as on pure market economics; check current search results for relevant critical minerals policy developments, since this space moves quickly.

**Common evaluation pitfalls**: quoting TREO% or contained-tonnage headlines without magnet-REE (Nd-Pr, and especially Dy-Tb) breakdown; not addressing where separation/processing will actually happen and at what cost/value-capture; treating REE market pricing as a single "REE price" rather than a basket of very differently-priced elements.
