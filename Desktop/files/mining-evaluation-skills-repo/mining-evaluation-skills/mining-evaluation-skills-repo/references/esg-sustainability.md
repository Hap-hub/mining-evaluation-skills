# ESG, Sustainability & Mining Method Selection

## Why this matters to the evaluation, not just as a compliance section

For both audiences this skill serves, ESG factors are increasingly financially material, not a side issue: permitting timelines, access to project financing (many lenders now apply ESG screens or require alignment with frameworks like the Equator Principles or ICMM's Mining Principles), and social license to operate all directly affect schedule and cost — which flow straight into NPV. Treat ESG analysis as an input to the economic/risk case, not a separate box-ticking exercise appended at the end.

## Mining method selection

The choice between open pit and underground (and, within underground, method — block caving, sublevel stoping, cut-and-fill, etc.) is driven by:
- Deposit geometry (depth, dip, tabular vs. massive) and strip ratio economics for open pit options.
- Geotechnical conditions (rock competency, hydrogeology).
- Grade distribution — underground methods generally require higher cut-off grades to be economic given higher mining unit costs, so a deposit with a large low-grade halo may only be economic as an open pit even if higher-grade underground extensions exist.
- Environmental/social footprint — open pit typically has a larger surface disturbance footprint; underground often has a smaller footprint but different risks (subsidence, water management).

When a user's study proposes a mining method, sanity-check it against grade/geometry rather than taking the choice as given — a method mismatch (e.g., proposing open pit for a narrow, steeply-dipping high-grade vein deposit) is a common weak point worth flagging.

## Core ESG evaluation areas

- **Environmental**: water balance and consumption, tailings storage facility design and closure planning, biodiversity/land disturbance, air quality (dust, emissions), energy source and carbon intensity.
- **Social**: community and Indigenous consultation and consent processes, resettlement (if any), local employment and procurement commitments, artisanal/small-scale mining overlap (a significant issue for several commodities, notably cobalt in the DRC).
- **Governance**: transparency of ownership/beneficial interests, anti-corruption controls, jurisdictional governance risk (Resource Governance Index and similar indices are useful reference points, though Claude should note current scores via search rather than relying on memory, since these shift).

## Sustainability reporting frameworks worth knowing

- **ICMM Mining Principles** — voluntary framework for member companies covering environmental/social performance.
- **TSM (Towards Sustainable Mining)** — Canadian-originated, now adopted internationally, with public performance scorecards.
- **IRMA (Initiative for Responsible Mining Assurance)** — independent, multi-stakeholder assurance standard, generally considered more rigorous/third-party-verified than company-run schemes.
- **Equator Principles** — risk management framework used by project finance lenders to assess environmental/social risk.

## Practical guidance for Claude

When asked to evaluate a project's ESG profile and the user hasn't supplied specifics, don't generate a generic "the project should engage stakeholders and minimize environmental impact" paragraph — that's not useful to either audience. Instead, ask what's known about the specific project's water source, tailings plan, and community context, or clearly scope the response as a framework/checklist (what should be disclosed) rather than an assessment (how well the project is doing) if the underlying facts aren't available.
