# Nickel — Evaluation Notes

**Two fundamentally different deposit types with very different economics — always identify which one first:**

**1. Sulphide nickel** (e.g., Sudbury-style, komatiite-hosted): typically higher grade (~1–3% Ni common), conventional flotation processing to concentrate, often carries valuable by-product credits (Cu, Co, PGEs — platinum group elements), and can feed directly into conventional smelters. Generally the lower-capex, lower-technical-risk route to market.

**2. Laterite nickel** (weathered tropical/sub-tropical ore, e.g., Indonesia, Philippines, New Caledonia): typically lower grade (~1–2% Ni or less) but enormous tonnage; requires either:
   - **HPAL (High Pressure Acid Leach)** — produces battery-suitable nickel (nickel sulphate/MHP/MSP intermediate products); historically plagued by cost overruns and technical/commissioning problems industry-wide — treat HPAL capex/schedule estimates with real skepticism and benchmark against actual peer project outcomes (not just the peer projects' original estimates, which were often badly missed) via search.
   - **RKEF (Rotary Kiln-Electric Furnace)** — produces nickel pig iron (NPI) or ferronickel, mainly for stainless steel, not battery-grade — a much more proven, lower-capex-risk route but produces a different, lower-value product than HPAL's battery-suitable output.

**Grade context matters enormously for cost comparison** — a laterite project's low headline grade is not comparable to a sulphide project's grade without also comparing processing route, since laterite processing (especially HPAL) carries structurally higher capex/opex per tonne of contained nickel than sulphide flotation.

**Market factors**: nickel has bifurcated demand — stainless steel (majority of historical demand, served well by NPI/ferronickel from RKEF) versus battery/EV demand (needs Class 1 nickel or nickel sulphate, historically from sulphide refining or HPAL). A project's product form (NPI vs. ferronickel vs. nickel sulphate vs. Class 1 metal) determines which demand pool and price benchmark is actually relevant — don't evaluate a project against a generic "nickel price" without checking which market it actually sells into. Indonesian supply growth (RKEF-based NPI, increasingly also HPAL) has been a major driver of recent nickel market dynamics — worth a current search check given how fast this has moved.

**Common evaluation pitfalls**: comparing a laterite project's low grade directly against a sulphide project's grade without adjusting for processing route cost differences; assuming HPAL capex/schedule estimates will be met without benchmarking against the sector's actual track record; not identifying which nickel product form (and therefore which price benchmark) a project actually produces.
