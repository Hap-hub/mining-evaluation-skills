# Gold — Evaluation Notes

**Deposit types**: orogenic/lode gold (structurally-controlled veins), epithermal (high- and low-sulphidation), intrusion-related, Carlin-type (sediment-hosted disseminated, Nevada-style), placer/alluvial, and gold as a by-product of porphyry copper.

**Typical grades**: highly variable by deposit type — open-pit bulk-tonnage deposits can be economic at <1 g/t Au given scale; narrow high-grade underground vein deposits may run 5–15+ g/t Au. There's no single "typical" grade — always benchmark against the specific deposit type, not a generic number.

**Nugget effect / sample representativity**: gold, especially coarse or structurally-controlled gold, is prone to the "nugget effect" — high local grade variability that makes sample-based grade estimation unusually error-prone. When reviewing gold assay/resource data, check sample size (small-diameter core can underrepresent coarse gold) and whether metallic screen assays or duplicate/check sampling were used to validate high-grade intercepts. A single spectacular high-grade intercept without supporting continuity is a classic gold-project red flag.

**Processing**: free-milling ore → cyanide leach (CIL/CIP) is standard and relatively low-capex/low-risk; refractory ore (gold locked in sulphides, e.g., pyrite/arsenopyrite) requires pre-treatment (roasting, pressure oxidation, or bio-oxidation) — materially higher capex/opex and technical risk. Always check whether a project's ore is free-milling or refractory; this single fact drives much of the processing capex estimate.

**Market factors**: gold is comparatively insulated from industrial-demand-cycle risk relative to base metals (investment/central-bank demand is a large share of demand), so price-deck sensitivity discussions for gold projects often focus more on the study's price assumption relative to spot/consensus forward curves than on demand-side volume risk.

**Common evaluation pitfalls**: high-grade underground gold projects sometimes understate dilution (waste rock mixed in during mining lowers effective mill-feed grade below the resource grade) — check whether the study's mine plan applies a realistic dilution factor to the resource grade rather than using undiluted resource grade as the mill-feed assumption.
