# Lead-Zinc — Evaluation Notes

**Almost always evaluated together** because they occur together geologically far more often than not — treat a lead-zinc project as inherently polymetallic (frequently with significant silver, and sometimes copper/gold credits) rather than evaluating either metal in isolation. A single-metal Pb or Zn economic case that ignores meaningful by-product credits is likely understating project economics.

**Deposit types**: SEDEX (sediment-exposed exhalative — large, stratiform, e.g., some of the largest Zn-Pb-Ag deposits globally), VMS (volcanogenic massive sulphide, often also carrying Cu/Au), and MVT (Mississippi Valley-Type — carbonate-hosted, typically lower-temperature, structurally simpler, often lower grade but large tonnage).

**Typical grades**: combined Pb+Zn grades of 5–15% are common in economic deposits, with SEDEX deposits sometimes reaching higher combined grades; MVT deposits tend toward lower grade, higher tonnage.

**Processing**: differential flotation to separate lead concentrate and zinc concentrate as distinct products (they report to different smelters with different specifications) — a project's flowsheet should show separate Pb and Zn concentrate streams with their own grade/recovery/payability assumptions; a model that lumps Pb+Zn into one blended concentrate stream is almost always wrong for this commodity pair.

**Market factors**: like copper, TC/RCs (treatment and refining charges) for Pb and Zn concentrates matter materially to producer economics and are separately negotiated/benchmarked from copper terms — check these are modeled with commodity-appropriate terms, not copied from a copper assumption set. Silver by-product credit is frequently a significant swing factor in Pb-Zn project economics and should be explicitly modeled, not ignored.

**Environmental note**: lead concentrate handling and lead-bearing tailings carry elevated environmental/health scrutiny (lead is a well-established human health hazard) — permitting and community engagement risk for Pb-bearing projects is often higher than for a comparable Zn-only or Cu project, worth flagging explicitly in a risk assessment.

**Common evaluation pitfalls**: ignoring silver/by-product credits; not modeling Pb and Zn concentrates as separate product streams with separate market terms; underestimating the elevated ESG/permitting scrutiny lead-bearing projects typically face relative to other base metals.
